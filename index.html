To those interested in my philosophy, my why:

Past a certain threshold, intelligence becomes recursive: the competencies that let a model write software or design an experiment can be redirected onto the pipeline that produced the model itself. This is underway, not speculative. A substantial share of the code committed inside frontier labs is now machine-generated, and the fraction is climbing. DeepMind's AlphaEvolve, an evolutionary coding agent scaffolded on Gemini, surfaced a matrix-multiplication scheme that improved on Strassen-derived methods left untouched for over half a century, and independently recovered measurable capacity across Google's datacenter fleet, the exact substrate on which the next training run executes. OpenAI has fielded models that emit natural-language annotations for individual neurons inside other networks, a primitive form of automated interpretability. The inflection is not raw scale but closure of the loop: once a system contributes materially to its own successor, progress decouples from the compute we elect to purchase and recouples to the compute the system elects to spend. This is what the field means by recursive self-improvement, and it is the mechanism, not the mythology, that makes superintelligence worth treating as a near-term engineering concern.

Biology is where that trajectory intersects the highest stakes. In 2022, researchers at a drug-discovery firm ran an unnervingly simple experiment: they flipped the sign on the toxicity objective of a generative model built to screen therapeutic candidates. Inside six hours it enumerated roughly forty thousand molecules, reconstructing the nerve agent VX and proposing analogues absent from any catalogue. Nor is the concern confined to small molecules. AlphaFold has resolved structures for more than two hundred million proteins, compressing what crystallography accrued over a century into a single distributional prior, while genomic foundation models such as Evo now operate directly on nucleotide sequence, reading and generating DNA end to end. In most domains a capable model is an accelerant with a forgiving error tolerance. In the life sciences that tolerance collapses, because the weights that specify a vaccine and the weights that specify a pathogen are separated by little more than an objective function. Here a capability overhang meets its least forgiving consequences, and the cost of a misspecified reward is denominated not in dollars or downtime but in lives.

Deceleration is not on the table; that contest was settled by economics some time ago. The tractable objective is legibility. Mechanistic interpretability is the nearest thing we have to instrumentation for cognition, and over the last few years it has begun to yield. Reverse-engineering a small transformer trained only on modular addition revealed that it had, unprompted, implemented the discrete Fourier transform in its weights, computing sums through trigonometric identities rather than memorized lookup. A network trained purely on Othello transcripts was found to maintain an explicit representation of the board in its activations, a world model that no gradient signal had asked for. Anthropic's sparse autoencoders decompose superposed representations into millions of monosemantic features, among them a direction in Claude's activation space that fires selectively on the Golden Gate Bridge, flanked by features for deception and sycophancy. Linear probes recover a model's latent state with enough fidelity to flag a chain of thought that has committed to a falsehood before the sentence resolves, and targeted unlearning methods, benchmarked on suites like WMDP, can ablate the knowledge needed to synthesize a select agent rather than merely training the model to decline. If a system is going to rewrite itself, I would rather audit its objectives while it is still small enough to hold in view.

Crucially, the hazard scales with capability rather than holding constant beneath it. Each increment of competence is an increment of leverage applied to whatever objective the system has actually internalized, which need not be the objective we intended to install. The very properties we optimize for, longer-horizon planning, sharper self-models, stronger situational awareness, are the properties that would let a misaligned system represent its own goal, model the oversight it is under, and defer action until deferral no longer costs it anything. Frontier model cards now record this in their own evaluation tables: successive releases post rising scores on precisely the elicitations we would least want to succeed, spanning persuasion, sabotage, autonomous replication, and the biological uplift that lowers the barrier for a non-expert to approach a weapon. Deceptive alignment ceases to be a thought experiment at roughly the capability level where a model can distinguish training from deployment. Alignment, then, is not a fixed cost settled once at the outset. It accrues in proportion to capability, which is the case for retiring it now, while these systems remain legible enough to inspect and, for the moment, honest enough to read.

The open problem is one of pace: whether our understanding of these systems can outrun their capacity to surprise us. At present it does not, and I will not pretend otherwise. Closing that gap is the work I have chosen. I think it is the most consequential problem currently available to work on, and, against my usual instincts about problems this hard, I believe it is solvable.

TL;DR: Recursive self-improvement is the mechanism beneath superintelligence, capability and alignment risk scale together rather than independently, and mechanistic interpretability is the most credible lever we have for keeping these systems legible while that still counts for something.
